Electricity plans

The EnergySage guide to home electrification

Written by Liam McCabe · Updated July 1, 2024 · 8 min read

Home electrification means upgrading your home to run entirely on electricity rather than fossil fuels like gas, propane, or oil. It's becoming more common — and more affordable — thanks to advances in technology, federal incentives, and rising interest in energy independence.

Over a quarter of all U.S. households now have all-electric major appliances, according to the Department of Energy.

Heat pumps outsold gas furnaces in the U.S. for the first time in 2023. Nearly 5% of homes have rooftop solar, and over 1 million electric vehicles were sold in the U.S. last year. Home electrification isn't a niche trend — it's becoming mainstream.

Why switch to an all-electric home?

There are several compelling reasons homeowners are making the switch:

  • Energy independence. Solar panels paired with a home battery can reduce your reliance on the utility grid.
  • Potential cost savings. Federal and state incentives can significantly offset upfront costs, and electric appliances often cost less to operate.
  • Improved air quality. Eliminating gas appliances removes sources of carbon monoxide and nitrogen dioxide from your home.
  • Better performance. Heat pumps, induction ranges, and EVs often outperform their fossil-fuel counterparts.
  • Lower environmental impact. Even with grid electricity, all-electric homes typically produce fewer emissions than gas-powered ones.

What can you electrify?

Almost everything in your home can run on electricity. The biggest opportunities are:

  • Heating and cooling — heat pumps replace both furnaces and central air conditioners
  • Water heating — heat pump water heaters are 2–3x more efficient than resistance electric models
  • Cooking — induction ranges heat faster and more precisely than gas
  • Transportation — EVs paired with home charging eliminate trips to the gas station
  • Solar and storage — rooftop solar "supercharges" the benefits of every other upgrade

What's the right order to go electric?

Start with a home energy audit. Many states offer free or subsidized audits that identify where to improve insulation, seal air leaks, and assess your electrical panel capacity before you start adding new electric loads.

The most cost-effective approach for most households is to electrify appliances as they reach end of life — rather than replacing functioning equipment early. The exception: if you're currently heating with propane or resistance electric heat, switching to a heat pump right away is likely to pay off quickly.
